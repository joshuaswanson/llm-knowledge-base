# Abstract Class

In the context of [[Knowledge Graph]]s and [[Knowledge Representation]], an [[Abstract Class]] is a conceptual structure used to define relations and entities within a [[Schema]]. While often associated with object-oriented programming in broader computing contexts, within the provided knowledge base framework, the term appears specifically in the definitions of flexible relations for knowledge graphs.

## Role in Knowledge Representation

An [[Abstract Class]] functions as a component in the schema layer of a [[Knowledge Graph]]. It assists in defining the structure of data and the relationships between [[Entity]]s. According to definitions viewed through a [[Semantic Web]] lens, key features of knowledge graphs include:

*   **Flexible Relations:** Defines [[Abstract Class]]es and relations of entities in a schema.
*   **General Structure:** A network of entities, their semantic types, properties, and relationships.
*   **Supporting Reasoning:** Acquires and integrates information into an [[Ontology]].[^19]

## Schema and Relations

The use of [[Abstract Class]]es allows systems to interrelate arbitrary entities and cover various topical domains. This abstraction is crucial for organizing data within graph-based repositories such as [[DBpedia]], [[Freebase]], and [[Google Knowledge Graph]].

### Implementation Context
[[Abstract Class]]es are part of the schema vocabulary that organizes data in graph databases and virtual knowledge graphs. They enable:
*   **Interlinking:** Connecting objects, events, situations, or abstract concepts.
*   **Semantics:** Encoding free-form semantics underlying entities.
*   **Schema Organization:** Often utilized alongside standards like [[Schema.org]], [[RDFa]], and [[Microdata]].[^10][^11]

## Definitions

There is no single commonly accepted definition of an [[Abstract Class]] outside of its specific usage within schema definitions for knowledge systems. A simpler definition of the surrounding structure is:

> "A digital structure that represents knowledge as concepts and the relationships between them (facts). A knowledge graph can include an ontology that allows both humans and machines to understand and reason about its contents."[^22][^23]
